College Essay: Dave Matthews Band Experience

  Twenty thousand people sit in a dull roar. I wait anxiously, playing on my phone to pass time. The lights in the Garden dim. The room is overcome with an eruption of applause. I stand and cheer too. A group of seven individuals take the stage and begin noodling on their instruments, toying with everyone. It is as if they have their hands on the volume dial of the crowd, turning it up steadily to a deafening level. My excitement builds. As the members of the Dave Matthews Band simultaneously hit the first note of the song “#41,” all stresses and concerns vacate my mind.

  I am a relatively busy person, but when twenty thousand fans sing so loudly that you can hardly hear Dave, there isn’t anything else to contemplate. Yes, I have a test on 1984 next week. I read seventy pages on the train ride up. Yes, I have an English essay due and a Latin test soon. The essay was finished on Thursday and the studying will be done on Sunday. Yes, I am employed, but I took the weekend off well in advance. Everything is planned out so I have no concerns during the concert.

  They segue out of “#41” into “Say Goodbye,” something the band has not done in fourteen years. I know this because I have studied the band’s touring history for as long as I have researched Tanzania for my term paper. I am able to use the skills I developed in class to facilitate my discovery of rare song performances, trends, and special shows. I will be the first to say that I have thoroughly analyzed enough literature in AP English and meticulously performed enough chi-squared goodness of fit tests in AP Statistics to award myself with discoveries I find more interesting.

  Dave strums the opening chords of “Christmas Song.” It is the first time he has done this mid-set in fifteen years; normally, it is played during the encore and infrequently at that. Consequently, he twists up the figurative crowd volume knob. As he flubs the first line and stops the song in laughter, the rest of the band laughs with him and bursts seamlessly into an improvised jam. The band’s talent impresses me yet again. Even those who barely know me know I love the Dave Matthews Band. It is a reputation I value as much as I value being in the fourth in my class or getting a 730 on the critical reading portion of the SATs my first try. I understand that the equal placement of my fandom among my academic reputations may seem incongruous; however, I want people to know that I am not another part of the high school herd, sitting idly with my face in my books and bending with each passing trend. Granted, my taste in music is not the most groundbreaking statement to make, but it defines me more than any test score.

  Two short hours later, the concert ends and I feel nostalgic. I am beyond satisfied with my fourth show of the year. I walk back to Grand Central with a grin, ringing ears, and a raw throat. I managed all my commitments to make these shows work for me. Now, it is time to return to normalcy. In just one day, I will be in class listening to my teachers instruct me about logarithmic functions and natural selection. In just one week, I will be running over ten miles in preparation for a future marathon. That was November 13th, 2010. Today, I have the lasting memories, the show in my iTunes, and the tickets on my desk. When the Dave Matthews Band comes by again, I’ll be there. The only difference is that next time, instead of studying Latin early at home, I’ll be troubleshooting advanced computer software early in my dorm.
